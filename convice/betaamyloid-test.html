Beta Amyloid 42/40 Ratio Testing

Beta-amyloid testing is a medical procedure used to detect the presence and accumulation of beta-amyloid protein in the brain, which is associated with Alzheimer's disease. Alzheimer's disease is characterized by the formation of abnormal protein deposits, including beta-amyloid plaques and tau tangles, in the brain. Beta-amyloid is a protein fragment that aggregates to form plaques between nerve cells in the brain.

Beta-amyloid testing can be conducted through various methods, including:
  1. Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Testing: This involves collecting a sample of cerebrospinal fluid through a lumbar puncture (spinal tap). The fluid is then analyzed to measure the levels of beta-amyloid and other biomarkers associated with Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Positron Emission Tomography (PET) Imaging: PET scans use a radioactive tracer that binds to beta-amyloid in the brain. The distribution of the tracer provides information about the presence and extent of beta-amyloid plaques in the brain.
  3. Beta-amyloid 42/40 Ratio Blood Testing: These blood tests use special lab equipment to directly measure trace amounts of beta-amyloid in blood samples. The beta-amyloid 42/40 ratio, combined with a person’s age and APOE genotype, determines an amyloid probability score. The score is sorted into ranges indicating a low, intermediate, or high likelihood of beta-amyloid plaques consistent with an Alzheimer’s diagnosis. We offer this blood test in our office.


The presence of beta-amyloid in the brain is a common feature of Alzheimer's, but not everyone with beta-amyloid plaques will necessarily develop the disease. Additionally, some individuals without Alzheimer's may have beta-amyloid accumulation. In some cases, beta-amyloid testing can help with diagnosing Alzheimer's disease when used in conjunction with other clinical assessments, cognitive evaluations, and imaging techniques. However, it's important to note that the definitive diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease typically relies on a comprehensive evaluation by a healthcare professional, considering the person's medical history, cognitive testing, physical examination, and other diagnostic criteria.
